The Kidnapped Goddess

A troubled story

This is the story of a sculpture with a powerful beauty and an uncertain identity. She has been named in many different ways: Morgantina Venus, Getty Aphrodite, and nowadays is best known simply as Morgantina Goddess. In the next pages you will know a little more about the thousand-year old, adventurous life of this extraordinary marble woman but, who is she? Archeologists and academics still discuss about who this goddess is. The original denomination “Venus” is surely wrong, because nowadays the most confirmed theory see the sculpture as Demeter. To prove this last theory there is the fact that Enna was the most influential center of the Goddess cult. Demeter is the goddess of harvest and agriculture, the mother who presides over the natural cycle of seasons on earth. The myth of Demeter and Kore (Cerer and Persephone are the roman equivalent names) is one of the most fascinating in greek mythology (learn more about the Rape of Persephone), and according to the legend, it took place in Enna, in the central area of Sicily. The sculpture is today hosted by the archaelogical museum of Aidone, in the heart of Sicily. About the Museum

The beautiful sculpture is exhibited at the Archaelogical Museum of Aidone. It is housed in the former Monastery of the Capuchins, built between 1611 and 1613. It was opened in 1984 and it exhibits relics of over thirty years of excavations in the area of Morgantina, and from the hill of the Citadel (site on which it was originally founded the city in 1000 a.C.). It includes a variety of exhibition halls, ordered in both chronological and thematical criteria, which illustrate the history of the ancient city of Morgantina, from the Bronze Age to the Republican Roman period.
